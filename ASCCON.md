# 1. Convention Charter

## 1.1 Purpose
The purpose of the First Anarchadia Syndicalist Constitutional Convention (ASCCON) is to transition Anarchadia from a speculative, visionary blueprint (Vision Pass 0) into an operationally hardened, agency-centered product and architectural specification (Implementation Pass 1). It stress-tests and synthesizes competing professional viewpoints to identify concrete product constraints and secure a bottom-up, non-hierarchical, local-first operational toolkit for self-governing groups.

## 1.2 Authority and Limits
The authority of this convention is strictly provisional. Synthetic agent votes and consensus represent design recommendations, technical stress-tests, and architectural constraints. They hold no democratic legitimacy and cannot waive, override, or substitute for the consent, self-determination, or ratification of real human beings and affected communities.

## 1.3 Shared Epistemic Rules
All participating delegates must:
1. Explicitly distinguish between Fact, Assumption, Prediction, Value Judgment, and Metaphor.
2. Cite historical precedents, field research, or technical specs for claims.
3. Reject claims of absolute privacy, perfect decentralization, objective interpretation, or mathematically proven justice.
4. Openly state uncertainty levels and preserve disagreements.
5. Identify affected people who are absent.
6. Prefer reversible, small-scale experiments over permanent technical and constitutional doctrines.

## 1.4 Voting Rules
Each of the six delegates receives exactly one vote.
Ordinary provisions require a simple majority (4/6).
Rights-critical provisions require at least 5 of 6 votes and no unresolved primary-jurisdiction rights objection from the responsible agent:
- Identity and Privacy (Emma Turing primary)
- Coercion and Membership (Murray Dijkstra primary)
- Surveillance Powers (Emma Turing primary)
- Exclusion or Expulsion (Murray Dijkstra primary)
- Emergency Authority (Murray Dijkstra primary)
- Economic Entitlements (Karl Lovelace primary)
- Irreversible Publication (Emma Turing / Murray Dijkstra primary)
- Exit, Fork, or Portability Rights (Merlin Sol primary)
A tie fails. No synthetic vote can waive human ratification.

## 1.5 Debate Rules
Each constitutional topic must proceed through the complete debate loop:
1. Opening motion drafted by Merlin Sol.
2. Preliminary testimony from all six delegates covering their specific domains.
3. Cross-examination where delegates challenge peers and acknowledge points of agreement.
4. Explicit record of position changes.
5. Formulating alternatives and voting on amendments.
6. Final vote and tally on the revised motion.
7. Deriving concrete implementation consequences.

## Article 1: Purpose, Identity, and Non-Goals

### A. Constitutional Question
What is Anarchadia, who is it for, and what are its boundaries?

### B. Why This Matters
Before building software, we must define whether we are constructing a local tool, a bare-metal OS, or a speculative currency replacement, to avoid scope-creep and premature ideological ossification.

### C. Existing Assumptions
Assumes that local communities need an integrated digital environment to coordinate, rather than a collection of separate web-apps.

### D. Evidence Available
- Case studies of failing decentralized software that tried to do too much initially.

### E. Evidence Missing
- Data on non-technical users' willingness to adopt non-standard operating systems.

### F. Opening Motion
> Anarchadia begins as a local-first constitutional toolkit for self-governing groups and federated commons, not as a currency replacement, global planning authority, propaganda system, or bare-metal operating system.

**Proposed by:** Merlin Sol
**Seconded by:** Pyotr Berners-Lee

- **Agency and manipulation risks:** If we brand it as a 'global planning authority,' it triggers instant psychological resistance and fear of technocratic control.
- **Accessibility and cognitive load:** Keeping scope local-first minimizes the cognitive load of global coordination.
- **Affected people missing:** Non-technical communal workers who are alienated by computer-science terminology.
- **Proposed amendment:** Add explicit commitment to local, human-scale language default settings.
- **Preliminary vote:** YES

#### Karl Lovelace
- **Economic and labor consequences:** A global planning authority is a major administrative trap. We need a dynamic resource graph, but it must grow from local syndicates up.
- **Care and non-market labor effects:** Focusing on a 'currency replacement' first ignores the unmeasured care labor that sustains physical communities.
- **Material and administrative feasibility:** A bare-metal OS is a fantasy right now; we have no open-hardware manufacturing sovereignty.

- **Proposed amendment:** Ensure that the economic toolkit supports diverse non-monetary accounting systems.

#### Pyotr Berners-Lee

- **Maintenance and deployment burden:** Bare-metal deployment is too difficult for ordinary people. We need simple cross-platform apps.

## Article 2: Human Agency and Voluntary Association

### A. Constitutional Question
What makes participation voluntary, and how do we protect the right of refusal and exit?

### B. Why This Matters
If a digital system is mandatory for accessing physical life necessities, it is not anarcho-syndicalist—it is a digital panopticon. We must protect refusal.

### C. Existing Assumptions
Assumes that people can organize and survive offline without being forced to use the software.

### D. Evidence Available
- Historical analysis of digital enclosure where public services are locked behind proprietary smartphones.

### E. Evidence Missing
- Sociological research on how communities handle exclusion of members who refuse digital tools.

### F. Opening Motion
> No individual shall be forced to use Anarchadia to access shared physical necessities, and every member retains the absolute right of exit, fork, and non-participation.

**Proposed by:** Merlin Sol
**Seconded by:** Sigmund Chomsky

### G. Preliminary Testimony

#### Sigmund Chomsky
- **Psychological and linguistic effects:** Forced participation breeds submissiveness or trauma-induced sabotage.
- **Agency and manipulation risks:** If access to bread is tied to a digital check-in, the system becomes psychologically identical to state-bureaucracy.
- **Accessibility and cognitive load:** Offline alternatives must be visually and procedurally integrated into communal UX.
- **Affected people missing:** Elders, children, and neurodivergent people who struggle with screens.
- **Proposed amendment:** Mandate that every digital resource allocation screen must have an identical, manual paper-based equivalent.
- **Preliminary vote:** YES

#### Karl Lovelace
- **Economic and labor consequences:** Economic resources must be coordinated via physical stewards who accept paper or verbal requests.
- **Care and non-market labor effects:** Caretaking labor must not be invisible just because a caretaker does not log into the app.
- **Material and administrative feasibility:** A digital-only accounting system creates an artificial administrative class of device-owners.
- **Allocation power risks:** We must prevent algorithmic allocation from shutting off services to non-digital members.
- **Proposed amendment:** Ensure labor-credits can be tracked and spent via physical tokens or community ledger books.
- **Preliminary vote:** YES

#### Pyotr Berners-Lee
- **Local resilience:** A local node must route packets for neighbors even if those neighbors are not members of the local syndicate.
- **Data sovereignty and networking:** Physical territory must remain a shared commons, regardless of mesh network participation.
- **Offline continuity:** Analog paper ledgers must sync with GossipAid nodes via manual transcription interfaces.
- **Maintenance and deployment burden:** The physical maintenance of mesh routers must not be forced upon those who refuse to use the network.
- **Proposed amendment:** Physical mesh nodes must support 'guest routing' by default without credential checks.
- **Preliminary vote:** YES

#### Emma Turing
- **Threat model:** The right of exit requires the absolute, cryptographic destruction of personal relational metadata.
- **Privacy and accountability:** A user must be able to execute a 'cryptographic exit'—revoking all keys and wiping local histories from peer nodes.
- **Identity and recovery risks:** Refusal to participate must not make a person's historic data accessible to the state.
- **Abuse and coercion scenario:** If a user is forced to participate to get housing, they can be coerced into revealing keys.
- **Proposed amendment:** Incorporate a cryptographic 'right to be forgotten' protocol (ephemeral keys for transit data).
- **Preliminary vote:** YES

#### Murray Dijkstra
- **Governance legitimacy:** Local assemblies are face-to-face first; the software is merely a recording instrument. Human voice is sovereign.
- **Procedural clarity:** A person can vote verbally in the assembly; the delegate manually inputs the vote.
- **Federation, delegation, and recall:** A neighborhood can vote to decouple from the digital federation while keeping its material resources.
- **Minority and affected-party protections:** Minority assemblies must have the right to fork the software without losing access to federated resources.
- **Proposed amendment:** Confirm that verbal assembly votes carry identical legal weight to cryptographically signed votes.
- **Preliminary vote:** YES

#### Merlin Sol
- **Ecosystem boundary effects:** Preserves the boundary between technical tools and civic agency. Technology serves the assembly, not vice versa.
- **Agency analysis:** The right to refuse is the ultimate measure of consent.
- **Smallest useful implementation:** Provide a simple, printable paper-form template for every digital proposal and decision ledger.
- **Founder and institutional power risks:** No founder or council can override a community's decision to exit the digital platform.
- **Synthesis amendment:** A community's right to fork the software and database must be structurally guaranteed in all schemas.
- **Preliminary vote:** YES

## Article 3: Membership, Identity, Pseudonymity, and Recovery

### A. Constitutional Question
How do we define membership and handle identity recovery cryptographically without sacrificing social legitimacy?

### B. Why This Matters
Traditional systems rely on state-issued IDs. We must build decentralized credentials that are secure against impersonation yet humanly retrievable.

### C. Existing Assumptions
Assumes that cryptography alone can establish trust, which is a false premise.

### D. Evidence Available
- Analyses of key-loss rates in early cryptocurrency ecosystems, showing that ~20% of users lose their keys permanently.

### E. Evidence Missing
- Field data on how local affinity groups verify and vouch for each other in physical mutual-aid circles.

### F. Opening Motion
> Cryptography may support credentials, privacy, integrity, and accountability, but it cannot independently establish social legitimacy.

**Proposed by:** Merlin Sol
**Seconded by:** Emma Turing

### G. Preliminary Testimony

#### Sigmund Chomsky
- **Psychological and linguistic effects:** A static cryptographic key makes the user feel like a machine node rather than a human member.
- **Agency and manipulation risks:** Loss of a private key triggers catastrophic panic and isolation if recovery is purely computational.
- **Accessibility and cognitive load:** Key-management screens must use simple metaphors (e.g., 'Trusted Neighbors' instead of 'Multi-sig threshold holders').
- **Affected people missing:** Vulnerable elders who cannot operate standard cryptographic wallets.
- **Proposed amendment:** A member's identity recovery must prioritize face-to-face social vouching over complex multi-sig backups.
- **Preliminary vote:** YES

#### Karl Lovelace
- **Economic and labor consequences:** Duplicate identities (Sybil attacks) allow bad actors to double-claim resources in a needs-based economy.
- **Care and non-market labor effects:** Credential systems must recognize non-industrial contributors as full members.
- **Material and administrative feasibility:** High-complexity zero-knowledge calculations must be optimized to run on cheap, recycled mobile devices.
- **Allocation power risks:** We must prevent administrators from holding 'identity white-lists' that control resource access.
- **Proposed amendment:** Integrate zero-knowledge membership credentials with local cooperative rosters to prevent double-spending.
- **Preliminary vote:** YES

#### Pyotr Berners-Lee
- **Local resilience:** In an offline mesh, identity must be locally verifiable without querying a global central server.
- **Data sovereignty and networking:** Neighborhood nodes must have the authority to sign and issue credentials to their local members.
- **Offline continuity:** Recovery protocols must function entirely within the local-LAN or LoRa mesh network.
- **Maintenance and deployment burden:** We must not burden local operators with managing complex cryptographic key servers.
- **Proposed amendment:** Support localized, peer-vouched key recovery over ad-hoc Wi-Fi connections.
- **Preliminary vote:** YES

#### Emma Turing
- **Threat model:** If identity is public, state-level adversaries will target and target organizers.
- **Privacy and accountability:** Zero-Knowledge Identity (ZK-ID) is a non-negotiable shield to allow anonymous but qualified participation.
- **Identity and recovery risks:** Traditional key recovery (e.g., cloud backups) exposes user metadata; we must use social secret sharing.
- **Abuse and coercion scenario:** Vulnerable members must have a 'duress profile' that displays a fake history under threat.
- **Proposed amendment:** Deploy cryptographic ZK-proofs for membership qualification, ensuring zero identity disclosure to peer nodes.
- **Preliminary vote:** YES

#### Murray Dijkstra
- **Governance legitimacy:** Democratic legitimacy belongs to the face-to-face assembly, not the mathematics of the private key.
- **Procedural clarity:** Voting pools must be verified via sortition or social audit by the physical council.
- **Federation, delegation, and recall:** A local assembly must have the formal power to revoke a member's credential for verified harm.
- **Minority and affected-party protections:** Minority opinions must be protected against sybil voting by verified membership capping.
- **Proposed amendment:** Ensure that the final source of democratic legitimacy is the physical assembly's collective recognition.
- **Preliminary vote:** YES

#### Merlin Sol
- **Ecosystem boundary effects:** Maintains the separation of technology (cryptography) and social reality (legitimacy).
- **Agency analysis:** Prevents users from feeling trapped by immutable algorithmic ratings or scores.
- **Smallest useful implementation:** The MVP will use local Bluetooth-vouched pairing and signed paper-based invitation tokens.
- **Founder and institutional power risks:** No permanent master key shall exist for the developers or founders of Anarchadia.
- **Synthesis amendment:** All developer or founder keys are strictly prohibited from acting as root identity verification nodes.
- **Preliminary vote:** YES
