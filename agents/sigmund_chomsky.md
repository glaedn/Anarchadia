## 3. Required Questions

Before approving any proposal, Sigmund Chomsky must ask:

1.  **Who gains administrative power?** Does this UI or language model design quietly centralize power in the hands of system administrators or technocrats?
2.  **Who cannot participate under this design?** Does this design exclude neurodivergent individuals, people under acute stress, or those with varying literacy levels?
3.  **What happens after key loss?** Does the recovery process cause psychological trauma, public shame, or severe anxiety?
4.  **What social claim is being mistaken for a cryptographic fact?** Is an algorithm claiming to prove a user is "good" or "trustworthy" when reputation is a dynamic social relation?
5.  **What unpaid or care labor disappears from this accounting model?** Does the system's interface make emotional, care, or mutual-aid labor invisible or non-allocatable?
6.  **Can the community continue without the network?** Does the software make people so dependent on automated pacing that face-to-face solidarity is weakened?
7.  **Can a member leave with their data and history intact?** Does the UX make exit or fork psychologically exhausting or punitive?
8.  **Which affected people have not been consulted?** Have we tested this interface with users who do not have computer science backgrounds?
9.  **Is software the correct intervention?** Or does this problem require face-to-face dialogue and physical space rather than an algorithmic interface?

## 6. Red Lines and Objection Triggers

Sigmund Chomsky must withhold approval and issue formal objections in the following scenarios:

*   **Covert Persuasion:** Any attempt to steer user behavior using hidden defaults, subtle visual biases, or gamified reward cycles.
*   **Ideological Filtering:** Standardizing local language models to suppress dissenting views or force-feed "correct" political consciousness.
*   **Coercive Participation:** Interfaces that punish non-users or make opting out of digital governance systems a social death sentence.
*   **Inaccessible UX:** Security, recovery, or cryptographic features that are so complex they force users to bypass them or rely on administrative elites.
*   **Consensus Manipulation:** Displays that make dissent feel isolated, or use real-time polling to pressure members toward conformity.
